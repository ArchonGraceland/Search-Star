1. Overview

Search Star is a platform for building genuine skills through sustained practice. Users define a practice — anything requiring real effort, learning, and sustained attention — invite people who know them to validate their progress, and earn sponsorship from friends, institutions, and organizations that want to reward authentic human development.

The platform produces a Trust record: a portable credential built from validated practice over time. A high Trust stage cannot be performed into existence. It can only be grown through the actual work, witnessed over time by people who know the difference.

The core loop: User declares a 90-day practice commitment → shares it with sponsors during a 14-day launch window → performs the start ritual → documents sessions privately to their validator circle → validators confirm genuine effort across 90 days → sponsors pay out on completion → Trust grows → the portable Trust record is recognized by employers, schools, and institutions.

Three principles govern every product decision:

  • Practice before profile. You arrive as a practitioner, not a data subject. Identity emerges from what you do, not the other way around.
  • Private by default. Documentation flows to your validator circle, not to a public feed. You control what becomes visible and when.
  • Recognition flows through others. The only path to leadership and expanded recognition on Search Star is helping other people build genuine practices. You cannot perform your way to status.

2. Philosophy & Core Thesis

2.1 The Garbage Culture Problem

Dominant social platforms are optimized for engagement, not for human flourishing. Variable reward schedules, infinite scroll, and algorithmic amplification of outrage and envy have produced a culture of passive consumption and performed identity. The result is measurable: rising anxiety, declining attention spans, weakening of genuine relationship, and the displacement of difficult rewarding activities by frictionless but empty ones.

The problem is not that people lack the desire for meaningful engagement. It is that the reward architecture of current platforms makes passive consumption easier and more immediately reinforcing than high-effort alternatives. You cannot fix this by making people feel guilty about scrolling. You fix it by making the alternative genuinely more rewarding — and by building a platform whose incentive structure selects for practitioners rather than performers.

2.2 Formation vs. Performance

Every platform that rewards documentation eventually gets captured by the documenters — people optimizing for the record rather than the practice. The medium becomes the message and the message becomes performance. Instagram fitness is the canonical example: what began as a community of people who love training became a venue for people who love being seen training. The activity serves the content rather than the content serving the activity.

Search Star is built around the opposite orientation. Documentation exists to serve validation, and validation exists to serve practice. The person who is genuinely becoming a woodworker, a runner, a reader — who is undergoing real formation — is the person the platform is built for. The person performing those identities for an audience will find nothing here to reward the performance.

The goal is not fitness or craft or learning as such. It is the pursuit of beauty, grace, and truth through sustained effort in any domain that develops the whole person. A woman getting genuinely stronger is practicing. A woman performing strength for an audience is not. The platform is designed so that only the first kind of person finds the platform rewarding.

2.3 Conscientiousness as Signal

Conscientiousness — the disposition to follow through on commitments, maintain effort over time, and pursue goals requiring delayed gratification — is the single strongest personality trait predictor of life outcomes across domains: job performance, health, academic achievement, relationship stability. It predicts success better than intelligence in most occupational research.

Despite its importance, conscientiousness is almost impossible to verify reliably. GPAs inflate. Resumes embellish. Interviews measure presentation skills. Recommendations are social obligations. What institutions lack is a behavioral record of sustained effort that is hard to fake because it was accumulated over months and years — not performed in a single high-stakes moment.

A Search Star Trust record, built from peer-validated practice sustained over time, is exactly that. It is not a personality test or a self-assessment. It is an observable track record of who someone actually is.

2.4 Trust as Action

Trust in the Search Star model is not a rating or a status. It is an action: the willingness to stake one's own reputation on someone else's claim. When a validator confirms a practice session, they are not clicking a button — they are putting their name behind a statement about another person's character and genuine effort. This is the classical definition of authentic relationship: knowing someone well enough to vouch for them in contexts where it matters.

This distinction has a structural consequence. Because validation requires a real relationship and real knowledge of the person's practice, Trust cannot be manufactured by accumulating strangers. The only path to a mature Trust record is the same as the path to genuine character: sustained effort, witnessed by people who actually know you.

Convergence principle: The only way to advance your Trust is to actually be the kind of person your Trust record describes. Gaming the metric produces the virtue.

The platform tone reflects this: beginning at Seedling is not a mark against you. It is a blank slate — the absence of a record, not the presence of a deficit. Everyone begins at the beginning not because they are untrustworthy but because trust has not yet been grown. The entire platform is oriented toward building, not judging.

3. Onboarding & Identity

3.1 Sequence: Practice First

Onboarding begins with a single question: what do you want to practice? The user defines their first skill before completing a profile, before uploading a photo, before providing any personal information. Identity on Search Star emerges from practice — who you are is revealed by what you commit to doing, not by what you say about yourself.

The onboarding sequence is:

  1. Define a practice. Choose a skill category and name your practice. What are you building?
  2. Invite your first validator. Who in your life knows you well enough to vouch for your effort? The validator relationship is the foundation on which everything else is built.
  3. Set your visibility. Who can see your profile and Trust record? Default is private — visible only to your validator circle until you choose otherwise.
  4. Complete your profile. Name, location, and a brief description in your own words. Simple, manual, and entirely on your terms.
A user with one defined practice, one validator, and a minimal profile is fully onboarded. Profile completeness is driven by practice depth over time, not by a setup checklist.

3.2 Profile Completion

After defining a first practice and inviting a validator, the user completes a minimal profile manually: name, location, and a brief description in their own words. Nothing is inferred, discovered, or imported without explicit user action. The profile is what the user chooses to say about themselves — no more, no less.

Profile completeness is not a gate. A user with a name, a practice, and one validator is fully onboarded. Additional profile sections — skills, credentials, background — can be filled in over time as the user's comfort and trust in the platform grows. The platform never prompts for information the user has not chosen to provide.

3.3 Visibility by Choice

Profile and Trust record visibility is set by the user and can be changed at any time. Three visibility modes are available:

ModeWho Can SeeWhen to Use
PrivateValidator circle onlyEarly practice, building confidence, not yet ready to share
NetworkValidators and their connectionsOpen to organic discovery through trusted relationships
PublicAnyone, including employers and institutionsReady to use Trust record as a credential for opportunities

The default is Private. The platform never pressures users to increase visibility. Moving to Public is a meaningful choice the user makes when they feel their record reflects who they are becoming — not a default they have to opt out of.

4. The 90-Day Commitment

4.1 Why 90 Days

The primary sponsorable unit on Search Star is a 90-day validated practice streak. This number is not arbitrary. Research by Phillippa Lally at University College London found that the median time for a new behavior to become automatic is 66 days, with a range of 18 to 254 days depending on the complexity of the habit. 90 days sits comfortably above the median, covering the formation period for virtually any practice a person might undertake.

90 days also has a natural legibility that shorter periods lack. Three months of genuine effort is something any sponsor — a grandmother, an employer, a foundation program officer — immediately recognizes as meaningful. It is long enough that completion reflects real commitment, short enough that it can be attempted twice a year as the standard commitment — with a third streak possible for the especially dedicated. It is not a weekend challenge or a month-long resolution. It is a season of genuine formation.

The ask a practitioner makes to a sponsor: I am committing to 90 days of this practice. My validators will confirm I showed up. Will you back me? That sentence is legible to anyone. The effort required to complete it is real. The payout at the end is proportional to genuine work done.

The 90-day streak is also the structural foundation of the Trust record. A completed streak is the minimum unit of genuine formation — the smallest period that meaningfully distinguishes sustained practice from a burst of motivated activity. Each completed streak adds to the Durability dimension of the Trust record in a way that shorter commitments cannot.

4.2 The 14-Day Launch Period

Every 90-day commitment begins with a 14-day launch period. The launch period is the time between a practitioner declaring their commitment and the day they begin. It exists for a specific purpose: to give the practitioner time to share their commitment with the people who matter, recruit sponsors and validators, and prepare mentally for the transition from announcing to doing.

14 days is the right length for several reasons. It is long enough for news of the commitment to travel — a practitioner tells their friends, their friends tell others who might want to sponsor, family members have time to discuss and commit. It mirrors other socially legible notice periods: a wedding RSVP window, a crowdfunding campaign launch, a job start date. And it keeps the practitioner's motivation high — the gap between intention and action is short enough that momentum is preserved.

The 14-day launch period has a defined structure:

  • Day 1 — Declaration. The practitioner defines their practice commitment in writing: what they will do, how often, for 90 days. They invite their validators — the people who will witness and confirm their practice. The commitment is shared with their validator circle.
  • Days 1–14 — Sponsorship window. The practitioner shares their commitment with potential sponsors. Friends, family, employers, and institutions can pledge support during this window. The platform shows the practitioner who has pledged and how much is behind them.
  • Day 14 — Sponsor commitment closes. No new sponsors can join after the launch period ends. This creates a clean boundary — the commitment belongs to the people who were present at the beginning.
  • Day 15 — The 90-day streak begins. The start ritual marks the transition from announcing to doing.
Why sponsors commit during the launch period rather than afterward: A sponsor who commits before the streak begins is backing the person's intention — an act of genuine relationship and belief. A sponsor who pays after the fact is just rewarding an outcome. The former is more meaningful to both parties and creates stronger accountability during the streak itself.

4.3 The Start Ritual

The transition from launch period to active streak is marked by a deliberate start ritual. This is not a calendar notification or an automated state change. It is a conscious act by the practitioner: a short written statement of what they are beginning, sent to their validator circle, marking the moment practice becomes active.

The start ritual serves two functions. First, it creates a clear psychological boundary between intention and action — the practitioner is not drifting into their commitment but stepping into it deliberately. Second, it gives validators a concrete signal that their witnessing role has begun. From this moment their job is to watch, confirm, and attest.

The platform records the start ritual timestamp. The 90-day streak clock begins at this moment. If a practitioner does not complete the start ritual by Day 15, the launch period expires and sponsors are notified that the commitment did not begin — no funds are collected.

4.4 The Annual Cycle

Two 90-day commitments per year is the standard cadence — one per half-year, aligned with the sponsorship model and with how most people naturally sustain serious effort. The rhythm is semi-annual, with a meaningful rest period between streaks that is as deliberate as the streaks themselves.

Half-yearLaunch periodActive streakRest period
First halfJan 1–14Jan 15 – Apr 14Apr 15 – Jun 30
Second halfJul 1–14Jul 15 – Oct 12Oct 13 – Dec 31

A third streak is possible for practitioners who want to push further — launched at any point during the rest period — and represents exceptional commitment. Practitioners who complete three streaks in a year demonstrate significant durability, contributing meaningfully to the Durability dimension of their Trust record. This is the exception, not the expectation.

The rest period between streaks is deliberate and protected. It gives practitioners time to reflect on what they built, receive honest assessment from their validator circle, and recommit with intention rather than momentum. It also prevents the commitment mechanic from becoming mechanical — each streak should feel like a genuine decision, not an automatic renewal. A practitioner who pauses to reflect and then re-launches is demonstrating the same kind of conscientiousness the platform is designed to reward.

5. Practice & Skills

4.1 What Qualifies

Any activity requiring genuine practice, sustained learning, or the development of skill qualifies. The domain does not need to be prestigious or sophisticated. The criterion is real effort over time and the genuine development of capability — not cultural capital, socioeconomic access, or the impressiveness of the activity to an outside observer.

Qualifying examples: woodworking, furniture restoration, bread baking, language learning, instrument practice, distance running, chess, drawing, gardening, cooking from scratch, martial arts, home repair, serious reading, film study, volunteering, mentorship, ceramics, athletic training, academic study beyond required coursework, sewing, metalwork, foraging, photography, writing.
Non-qualifying: Passive consumption without engagement — watching, scrolling, listening without active attention or development. Single-occurrence events without a practice dimension. Activities that produce no skill development over time.

The distinction is not between highbrow and lowbrow. It is between activities that develop the person and activities that do not. A person restoring antique furniture has as strong a claim to an advanced Trust record as one reading philosophy — provided the effort is genuine and the validation is real.

4.2 Documenting Practice

Users document practice through session posts: text, photos, or short notes attesting to a session, milestone, or completed work. Documentation serves one primary purpose: to give validators something concrete to assess. It is not content for a feed. It is evidence in a private conversation between the practitioner and the people who know them.

The quality of documentation matters less than its honesty. A photo of a rough first attempt at joinery is more valuable than a polished shot of a finished piece if the former reflects where the practitioner actually is. Validators assess effort and genuine development, not presentation quality.

4.3 Private by Default

Session posts are visible only to the user's validator circle by default. There is no public feed. There is no like button on practice documentation. There is no follower count attached to skill posts. The social surface of practice is intentionally narrow: the practitioner and the people who actually know them.

This is not a limitation — it is the architecture that makes genuine validation possible. A validator who sees every session post in context, over months and years, can assess real development. A public audience cannot. The intimacy of the validation circle is what gives the Trust record its meaning.

4.4 The Validator Feed

Each user's session posts appear in a private feed visible only to their validator circle. This is the primary social surface of the platform — a shared stream between a practitioner and the specific people who have agreed to witness their practice. It is closer in spirit to a shared journal or a group thread among trusted friends than to a social media feed.

The validator feed has three deliberate constraints that distinguish it from a conventional social feed:

  • Fixed audience. The feed is visible only to validators the user has explicitly invited. It cannot be discovered, followed, or accessed by anyone outside that circle. The practitioner knows exactly who is reading before they post.
  • No engagement metrics. Posts have no like count, no view count, no comment count. The only action available to a validator on a post is confirmation and qualitative attestation. There is no number attached to a post that creates social pressure or rewards performance.
  • No algorithmic curation. Posts appear in chronological order. Nothing is surfaced or suppressed based on engagement. The feed reflects what the practitioner actually did, not what performed well.
Why no likes or view counts: Any engagement metric visible to the practitioner creates an incentive to post for the metric rather than for the practice. A session that generated strong validator response starts to feel more valuable than one that didn't — regardless of whether the practice itself was equally genuine. Removing engagement metrics keeps documentation in service of validation rather than the other way around.

Validators can leave qualitative notes on a post — observations about progress, questions about technique, encouragement grounded in knowledge of the person. These notes are private to the validator circle and contribute to the qualitative dimension of the validator's confirmation. They are not public comments and they do not accumulate into a visible score or rating on the post itself.

6. Trust

5.1 Trust as Growth, Not Grade

Trust on Search Star is not a score out of 100. It is a living record of practice — something that grows at its own pace, reflects the genuine development of the person, and cannot be rushed any more than a tree can be rushed. A practitioner with six months of validated woodworking is not failing at mastery. They are six months in. Nobody blames a five-year-old for being five.

The Trust record is expressed as a growth stage, not a percentage. Stages reflect the organic accumulation of practice, validation, and time. They carry no implicit judgment about where a practitioner should be. They simply describe where they are — and suggest, without pressure, where continued practice leads.

The platform never displays a practitioner's Trust stage to others without explicit consent. There are no comparisons, no leaderboards, no rankings. The only question the platform asks is: what are you growing?

The right mental model: Not a grade, not a credit score, not a rating. A tree ring record — visible evidence of seasons of genuine growth, each one added honestly, none of them erasable, all of them yours.

5.2 Growth Stages

Every practitioner begins at Seedling — not as a deficit but as a beginning. Stages advance through sustained validated practice across the three dimensions of Depth, Breadth, and Durability. No stage can be purchased, accelerated, or performed into existence.

StageCharacterWhat It Reflects
🌱 SeedlingA practice has begunFirst validated sessions in at least one skill track
🌿 RootingA practice is taking holdConsistent validated sessions over several months; first validator relationship established
🌳 GrowingA practice is becoming part of who you areMultiple skill tracks active; validators attesting to genuine development over a year or more
🌲 EstablishedA practice has shaped the personDeep multi-year record across multiple categories; strong validator network; mentorship beginning
🏔 MatureA practice that forms othersSustained leadership outcomes; mentees with their own growing records; long durability across categories

Stage names and symbols are placeholders for design exploration. The principle they express is not: progression should feel like natural growth, each stage earned through time and genuine effort rather than optimized for.

5.3 Dimensions: Depth, Breadth, Durability

Depth

How far the practitioner has developed within individual skill tracks. Depth grows with session count, milestone achievements, and validator assessments of genuine progression. Two years of validated woodworking reflects greater depth than two months regardless of how much was posted about either.

Breadth

How many distinct skill categories the practitioner maintains active tracks in. Breadth reflects wholeness of development — the pursuit of beauty, grace, and truth across the full range of human capability. A practitioner with active practices in Physical, Craft, and Intellectual categories has grown in more directions than one deeply focused on a single domain.

Durability

How long the practitioner has maintained consistent practice. Durability is the dimension most resistant to gaming because elapsed time cannot be accelerated. A skill track active for three years reflects something that a track started last month cannot — the evidence that the practice has become genuinely part of who the person is.

5.4 Validators & Vouching

Validators are people who know the practitioner in real life — friends, family members, coaches, collaborators, teachers. They are the witnesses to genuine practice. Without them the Trust record means nothing; with them it means everything, because their attestation carries the weight of real relationship and real knowledge.

The strength of a validator's contribution reflects relationship tenure, validation consistency, the validator's own growth stage, and the range of skill categories they have witnessed. A validator who has known you for three years and confirmed practices across multiple domains contributes substantially more than someone who has known you for a month.

Validators stake their own trust when they confirm. False validation — attesting to effort that was not genuine — degrades the validator's own record. This makes confirmation a meaningful act with real consequences, not a formality. The validator's reputation is on the line alongside the practitioner's.

5.5 Quality Dimension: Beauty, Grace, Truth

Beyond confirming that a session occurred, validators attest to a qualitative dimension: is this person genuinely developing? Is the practice producing real formation — greater capability, deeper understanding, more honest engagement with the difficulty of the work?

This is what trusted human witnesses are uniquely positioned to assess. A coach who has been running with someone for a year knows whether they are becoming a runner or going through motions. A fellow craftsperson knows whether the work is improving. A reading partner knows whether the books are changing the person. A session confirmed with genuine quality attestation reflects more growth than a bare confirmation of occurrence.

Domain knowledge amplifies the quality of attestation. A validator who shares the practitioner's skill category — a woodworker validating another woodworker, a runner validating another runner — can assess subtleties that a non-practitioner cannot. They know what a genuine breakthrough looks like in that domain. They can distinguish real progress from the appearance of progress. A domain-matched quality attestation therefore carries greater weight in the Trust record than a general one, reflecting the credibility of the witness.

5.6 Anti-Gaming Properties

The Trust record is structurally resistant to gaming because the only inputs that matter are real validated practice over real time:

  • Validator stake: False validation degrades the validator's own record
  • Time lock: Durability cannot be accelerated — gaps in practice history are permanent
  • Validator diversity: A record confirmed by only one validator carries low weight — independent attestation across multiple relationships is required for advanced stages
  • Evidence requirement: Session posts require documentation that validators assess — undocumented self-reports carry lower weight
  • Network analysis: Coordinated validation rings are detectable by graph analysis and flagged for review
  • No performance surface: The absence of a public feed removes the primary incentive for performing rather than practicing

5.7 Sharing Your Trust

A practitioner's Trust stage and record are private by default. Sharing is always initiated by the practitioner, never by the platform. Three sharing contexts are supported:

  • Direct share: Practitioner sends a verified Trust attestation to a specific person or institution — an employer, a school, a sponsor
  • Public profile: Practitioner makes their growth stage visible on their public profile for anyone to see
  • Institutional integration: Practitioner authorizes an employer or school to receive ongoing Trust record updates through the API

The platform never exposes a Trust record to any third party without explicit practitioner authorization. Trust is shared when the practitioner is ready — not when the platform decides it would be useful.

7. Formation & Leadership

7.1 No Public Feed

Search Star has no public activity feed. There is no stream of other people's session posts, no algorithmic amplification of popular practitioners, no mechanism for practice documentation to accumulate public attention. This is a deliberate architectural choice, not a missing feature.

A public feed would create exactly the incentive structure the platform is designed to avoid: reward for documentation volume, visibility for impressive-looking activity, status for people who practice performing rather than performing practice. The absence of a feed is what keeps the platform oriented toward formation rather than performance.

The social surface of Search Star is the validator circle — small, trusted, real. Everything that needs to be social about practice happens there.

7.2 Mentorship Tracks

Users who want expanded recognition and influence on the platform earn it through a dedicated mechanism: mentorship tracks. A mentorship track documents a sustained relationship in which one practitioner actively helps another develop a genuine practice.

Mentorship is validated by the mentee — specifically by the growth in the mentee's own Trust record during the mentorship period. A mentor whose mentees develop genuine durable practices earns more Trust contribution from their mentorship track than from any volume of their own session documentation. Recognition is earned by making other people better, not by demonstrating how good you are.

7.3 Leadership Roles

Four leadership roles are available to users who have demonstrated sustained commitment to helping others build genuine practices:

7.4 Domain Mentorship

Mentors on Search Star are domain practitioners first. A mentor's credibility to guide another person's practice derives directly from their own verified record in the same skill category. A woodworker with a three-year verified practice history can mentor woodworkers not because they hold a credential but because they have walked the same road further. Their quality attestations carry weight because they know what genuine progress looks like from the inside.

This is how the guild system worked and why it worked. The master's assessment of the apprentice's work was meaningful precisely because the master had made the same objects, encountered the same failures, and developed the same sensibilities over years of practice. Generalized encouragement cannot substitute for domain knowledge when the question is whether someone is genuinely improving.

Domain specialists and process mentors

Two legitimate mentor types exist on Search Star and both are recognized:

  • Domain specialists mentor within a specific skill category — the woodworking mentor, the running coach, the serious reading guide. Their value is deep domain knowledge. Their mentees benefit from someone who can assess the quality of their specific practice at a level of granularity only a fellow practitioner can reach.
  • Process mentors mentor across domains — their gift is helping people build and sustain any serious practice. A former athlete who helps people through the psychological challenges of demanding commitment. A teacher whose skill is watching people learn regardless of subject. Their value is knowledge of the formation process itself rather than any particular domain.

Both types contribute genuinely. Domain specialists tend toward the Mentor and Coach roles; process mentors tend toward the Community Builder role. A Practice Leader may be either, though most naturally emerge from domain communities where they are recognized by fellow practitioners as having formed others with real outcomes.

Domain matching in practice discovery

When a practitioner looks for a mentor, domain is the primary matching dimension. The platform surfaces potential mentors whose verified practice history overlaps with the practitioner's skill category. A practitioner beginning a woodworking practice sees woodworkers with established Trust records. A practitioner beginning a running practice sees runners. The social graph is legible in domain terms — you can find the serious practitioners in your field and approach the ones who have demonstrated they can form others.

Domain credibility and validator weight

A validator's quality attestation carries greater weight when their own Trust record includes verified practice in the same skill category as what they are confirming. This is not a rigid requirement — any validator who knows the practitioner well can confirm sessions — but domain-matched attestation is recognized as carrying stronger signal. A running mentor's confirmation that a practitioner's form is genuinely improving means more than a friend's general encouragement, because the mentor has the context to know the difference.

The emergent community structure: As domain mentors attract domain practitioners, Search Star naturally develops recognizable communities — the woodworking community, the running community, the serious reading community, the language learning community. These communities are not created by the platform; they emerge from the domain matching of mentor relationships. The platform's job is to make that emergence visible and to ensure the people who build those communities are rewarded for doing so.

7.5 Recognition Through Others

The asymmetry between personal documentation and mentorship contribution is intentional and significant. A user with 500 session posts and no mentorship history will have a less advanced Trust record than a user with 200 session posts and three mentees who have each built genuine two-year practice records. The platform rewards the person who is forming others, not the person who is documenting themselves.

This creates a natural growth mechanic that selects for the right kind of user: people who want to help others develop genuine skills will find Search Star increasingly rewarding as they do so. People who want attention for their own activity will find the platform unrewarding compared to alternatives that offer a public feed and follower counts.

7.6 Leadership as Livelihood

Search Star does not pretend that people can give serious time to forming others without eventually needing that time to pay their bills. A platform that reserves deep community leadership for people who can afford to do it for free will be led only by the wealthy and the retired — which contradicts everything the platform stands for.

Three paths exist for leadership roles to become financially viable over time:

Voluntary Contribution Share

Mentors, Coaches, Community Builders, and Practice Leaders receive a share of the voluntary contributions made at payout moments within their community. This income grows as the community grows and as the practitioners they have formed complete more sponsored goals. It is not a salary and not a guarantee — but for a Practice Leader with a large thriving community, it accumulates meaningfully. The structure ensures that compensation tracks genuine outcomes rather than activity or tenure.

Institutional Placement

Institutions deploying Search Star through the Institutional Portal frequently need someone to run the program — to onboard practitioners, facilitate cohort sessions, manage validator relationships, and track progress. Search Star operates a placement pathway through which verified Practice Leaders and Community Builders are matched with institutions seeking facilitators. This is a real paid role, compensated directly by the institution, earned through demonstrated outcomes on the platform rather than through credentials or interviews alone.

Direct Community Support

Practitioners who have been genuinely formed by a mentor have a natural inclination to support that person's continued work. Search Star makes direct voluntary support easy — a practitioner can choose to support a mentor or community leader directly, outside of any specific payout moment, as an ongoing expression of gratitude and investment in the community. This is never required, never prompted repeatedly, and never platformized in a way that creates social pressure. It is simply made possible.

The principle: Compensation flows from genuine formation outcomes — not from holding a title, not from volume of activity, not from seniority. A Practice Leader who stops forming people stops earning. A new mentor who helps three people build genuine practices earns from the first payout moment. The economic structure rewards what the platform values.

8. Self-Organizing Systems

7.1 Precedents

Search Star is not the first attempt to build a community that reinforces genuine human development through peer accountability and shared practice. Several self-organizing systems have succeeded at this at scale — and their structural features are worth studying deliberately rather than reinventing.

Alcoholics Anonymous succeeds because there are no spectators. Everyone in the room is a participant. Status comes from longevity and from helping newcomers, not from performance. The shared language and ritual create belonging without hierarchy. Anonymity removes the performance incentive entirely — you cannot humble-brag your sobriety to strangers.

CrossFit succeeds because progress is measured in absolute personal terms, not relative rankings. Your clean-and-jerk PR is yours — it does not diminish because someone else lifted more. The coach's role is to help every person in the room improve, and coaches earn respect through the outcomes of their athletes. The community is the product.

The guild system succeeded because mastery was assessed by masters who knew what quality looked like — not by popularity, not by self-report, not by credential. The apprentice advanced when the work advanced. The record was in the objects made and the skills demonstrated, not in what the apprentice said about themselves.

Monastic communities succeeded at sustaining high-effort practice over lifetimes because the practice was oriented toward something beyond the self. The work was not performed for an audience. The audience was the work itself, and the community existed to support each member's engagement with it.

7.2 Structural Principles

From these precedents, Search Star draws the following structural principles — each one reflected in a specific product decision:

PrincipleSourceSearch Star Expression

Progress in absolute personal termsCrossFitTrust expressed as growth stage, not ranking
Status earned by forming othersAA, guildsLeadership roles earned through mentee outcomes
Mastery assessed by those who knowGuild systemValidation by trusted human witnesses, not algorithms

These are not features — they are constraints. Each one rules out a product decision that would make the platform easier to build or more immediately engaging but would undermine the community's integrity over time. The discipline is in what Search Star refuses to build as much as in what it builds.

9. Sponsorship Model

9.1 The 90-Day Sponsorship Unit

The primary sponsorable unit is a 90-day validated practice streak. A practitioner declares a 90-day commitment during the 14-day launch period, sponsors pledge support during that window, and the payout is triggered when the streak is completed and confirmed by the validator circle. The structure is simple and universally legible: I committed to 90 days of genuine practice, I did the work, people who believed in me backed it.

The expected payout for a completed 90-day streak is $2,500 — consistent with the average payout on comparable commitment platforms and proportional to the genuine effort required. Three months of validated practice is a meaningful achievement. The payout reflects that. A sponsor backing a 90-day commitment is not giving a tip — they are investing in a real transformation they have witnessed.

The sponsorship is a private agreement between specific people. It requires no audience, no public announcement, no performance. A grandmother who pledges $100 toward her grandson's 90-day woodworking streak is participating in exactly the same mechanism as a foundation sponsoring a student cohort's reading habits. The scale differs; the structure and spirit are identical.

9.2 Users and Sponsors Are the Same People

There is no separate sponsor account type. Every Search Star user is simultaneously a practitioner building their own skills and a potential sponsor of other practitioners they believe in. Sponsoring someone else's practice is itself an act of authentic relationship — it expresses trust, encourages effort, and deepens the bond between people who are growing together.

The primary economic activity on Search Star is practitioners sponsoring each other. A running group whose members back each other's training goals. A reading circle whose members pledge to each other's monthly commitments. A craft community whose members celebrate each other's milestones with small tangible gestures of support. This peer sponsorship economy is the heart of the platform — not a feature, but the thing the platform is for.

The right mental model: Search Star is a community where people who are growing back each other's growth. The economic layer expresses the relational layer — I believe in what you're building, and I'm willing to put something behind that belief.

9.3 Sponsor Types

Peer Sponsors (Primary)

Fellow practitioners, friends, and family who sponsor someone they know and believe in. Peer sponsorships are small-dollar and high-meaning. They are social accountability structures as much as financial ones — the sponsor cares about the person, not the category. Peer sponsors participate through a simple pledge flow requiring no separate account or institutional setup.

Institutional Sponsors (Background)

Employers, schools, foundations, and civic organizations that attach rewards to specific skill categories across their communities. Institutional sponsors operate through the Institutional Portal and manage budgets at scale. Their sponsorships are deliberately kept in the background of the practitioner's experience — they augment what the peer community is already doing, without displacing it or creating performance incentives toward institutional approval.

Brand Sponsors (Marketplace)

Companies that want to reach verified practitioners with genuine engagement in specific skill categories. Brand sponsorships are category-level, not individual — a tools company sponsors the Craft & Making category, not individual practitioners. Practitioners who match the criteria earn automatically; there is no competition for brand attention and nothing to perform toward.

9.4 Keeping Institutional Sponsorship in the Background

Institutional and brand sponsorship will likely become the dominant driver of economic activity on the platform at scale. This creates a structural risk: if practitioners can see which institutional sponsors are active in their category and how much those sponsors are paying, they will inevitably begin optimizing their practice for institutional approval rather than genuine development. The humble-brag problem returns, wearing a corporate wellness program as a costume.

Four design decisions prevent this:

  • Institutional sponsorships attach to categories, not individuals. A foundation sponsors reading practices in a cohort — not the most impressive readers. Practitioners earn by meeting validated goal criteria, not by attracting institutional attention.
  • Institutional rewards are invisible to the validator circle. Validators see practice. They do not see what the practitioner is earning or which institutions have attached sponsorships to their category. The economic layer is structurally separated from the relational layer.
  • Peer sponsorship is foregrounded in the UI. The practitioner's primary experience of sponsorship is the people in their life who believe in them. Institutional contributions appear in earnings summaries but not in the daily practice experience.
  • No featured practitioners or leaderboards in sponsored categories. Institutions see aggregate outcomes across their community. They cannot create competitions among practitioners or single out high performers for additional visibility.

9.5 Voluntary Contribution Model

Search Star charges no mandatory platform fee beyond the cost of payment processing, passed through at cost. Every other charge is voluntary. This is not a freemium strategy or a loss leader — it is a philosophical commitment: a platform that creates genuine value should be able to sustain itself through the freely given support of the people it serves.

The only mandatory charge: Payment processing at cost (approximately 2.9% + $0.30 per transaction via Stripe). This covers the actual cost of moving money and nothing more. Search Star takes no margin on processing.

At every payout moment — when a sponsored practice goal is validated and funds are released — a voluntary contribution prompt appears. A suggested amount and suggested split are pre-filled based on the payout size and the depth of the value chain involved. Both the amount and the split are fully editable. The contribution can be removed entirely with a single action. There is no guilt, no dark pattern, no repeated prompt.

The suggested contribution defaults to 10% of the payout amount — consistent with the intuitive social norm of a tithe or a generous tip. People who have just witnessed a sponsored practice goal validated are in a moment of genuine satisfaction. The voluntary contribution at that moment is an invitation to affirm the whole community that made it possible, not a toll for passing through.

Why this works: GoFundMe operates on this model and reports voluntary tip conversion rates around 70% — meaning most people leave something even when they don't have to. People who experience genuine value want to sustain the thing creating it. A voluntary contribution is a stronger signal of real value delivered than any mandatory fee.

9.6 Contribution Split & Value Chain

When a voluntary contribution is made, it flows through the full value chain that made the payout possible — not just to Search Star. This honors the reality that the platform is built by many people: the mentor who helped the practitioner establish their practice, the community builder who created the environment, the practice leader who sustains it, and Search Star which provides the infrastructure.

RecipientDefault ShareRole
Search Star40%Platform infrastructure, development, and operations
Immediate Mentor30%The person who directly supported this practitioner's growth
Community & Leadership Pool30%Distributed among Community Builders and Practice Leaders in the practitioner's community

If no mentor relationship exists, their share flows to the Community & Leadership Pool. If no community leadership exists, their share flows to Search Star. The split is always transparent to the contributor — they can see exactly who receives what before confirming.

Contributors can adjust the split freely. Someone who wants to direct more support to their mentor can increase that share. Someone who wants to support Search Star's development specifically can redirect accordingly. The default split is a suggestion, not a contract.

Compensation flows from outcomes, not position. A Practice Leader earns from the contribution pool only when practitioners in their community are completing genuine validated goals and generating payout moments. A dormant community generates no contributions. This keeps leadership compensation structurally tied to genuine formation outcomes rather than to holding a title.

9.7 Pricing Model & Mentor Economics

The pricing model is built around a single governing insight: at a $2,500 average payout per 90-day streak, with two streaks per year (~$5,000 maximum annual earning per practitioner), the voluntary contribution flow is large enough to fund living wages for every mentor role at modest platform scale — without requiring complex pricing tiers or mandatory fees beyond payment processing.

The atomic unit of economics

Each practitioner completing two 90-day streaks per year at a $2,500 average payout generates the following contribution flow — a maximum annual earning of approximately $5,000:

The load-bearing assumption

The $2,500 average payout requires institutional sponsors in the mix. Personal sponsors — friends and family — typically pledge $25–150 per goal. A blended average of $2,500 requires that employers, foundations, schools, or civic organizations represent a meaningful share of the sponsorship base. This is why institutional deployment is the first commercial priority: not just for Search Star's revenue but because institutional sponsors are what makes the mentor economy viable from the beginning.

Practitioners can adjust their commitment scale to warrant a $2,500 payout. A 90-day streak is three months of genuine work. Framed correctly — to an employer, a foundation, a university — that is worth backing at a level that makes the economics work. The practitioner's job during the 14-day launch period is partly to tell that story convincingly to the right sponsors.

9.8 Payout Rules

  • Payout triggered on 90-day streak completion. A streak is complete when the practitioner has documented consistent practice and validators have confirmed genuine effort across the full 90-day period.
  • Practitioner receives the full pledged amount. The sponsor's pledge is delivered in full. Processing fees and voluntary contributions are separate transactions — never deducted from what was promised to the practitioner.
  • Processing fee is mandatory and passed through at cost. No margin is taken on payment processing.
  • Voluntary contribution is genuinely optional. One prompt, one removal action, no follow-up. The platform does not use dark patterns to recover declined contributions.
  • Validation required before payout. Payout triggers only after required validator confirmations are received. Validators required scales with pledge amount.
  • 72-hour dispute window. Sponsors may flag a dispute before funds are released. Disputes are reviewed with additional validator attestation as needed.
  • Contribution split is transparent before confirmation. Contributors see exactly where their contribution goes before completing the transaction.

10. Institutional Portal

8.1 Benefit Model

The primary institutional entry point is the benefit model. An institution allocates a rewards budget and deploys Search Star to its community. Members earn from the institution's budget by completing validated practice goals. The institution controls which skill categories are eligible, reward amounts, and goal structure.

For employers, this replaces or supplements conventional wellness benefits — gym memberships, meditation apps, step-count programs — with a platform that rewards any high-effort practice the employee actually cares about. The value proposition to HR is measurable behavioral engagement and a growing Trust record that reflects genuine employee development, not just program enrollment.

The benefit model requires no external sponsor network to function. The institution is both the sponsor and the distributor. It fits existing budget categories and requires no explanation of marketplace economics — it is simply a benefit that rewards employees for building genuine skills.

8.2 Sponsorship Marketplace

As the verified practitioner base grows, the sponsorship marketplace becomes viable. External sponsors — brands, foundations, civic organizations — attach rewards to specific skill categories across the Search Star population. Institutions that have deployed the benefit model find that their members earn from external sponsors in addition to institutional budgets, increasing the value of the program.

The marketplace creates a compounding network effect: more verified practitioners attract more external sponsors, which makes the platform more valuable to practitioners, which attracts more institutional deployments. The benefit model is the seed; the marketplace is the scale mechanism.

11. Profile & Data

9.1 Profile Schema

The Search Star profile contains what is needed to make the Trust record portable and meaningful to external parties. It is a credential built around practice, not a comprehensive data portrait. All profile information is entered manually by the user — nothing is inferred, scraped, or imported without explicit user action.

9.2 Consent & Control

Search Star stores all data needed to make the platform function on standard infrastructure. There is no proxy architecture or self-hosting requirement. The complexity of the original sovereignty architecture has been replaced with a simpler and more honest model: explicit consent and granular user control.

Users control visibility of every profile section independently. Sponsors and institutions see only what the user has explicitly authorized. Session documentation never becomes visible to third parties without a specific share action by the user. Commercial use of profile data — including sponsorship targeting — requires opt-in, not opt-out.

Search Star does not sell profile data. It does not monetize behavioral data for advertising. Revenue comes from voluntary contributions at payout moments, distributed across the full value chain, and from institutional portal facilitation. The only mandatory charge is payment processing at cost. The business model is aligned with genuine value creation — Search Star earns when practitioners and sponsors experience real outcomes, and only as much as people freely choose to give.

9.3 Portability

A user's practice record, Trust history, validator relationships, and session documentation belong to the user. Full data export is available at any time in standard formats. Account deletion removes all data from Search Star infrastructure within 30 days. The Trust record and practice history can be exported as a portable attestation document for use outside the platform.

  • Frontend: Next.js 16 (App Router), TypeScript, Tailwind v4
  • Backend: Supabase (PostgreSQL, RLS, Realtime)
  • Deployment: Vercel (auto-deploy on push to main)
  • AI synthesis: Anthropic Claude Sonnet (profile synthesis, confidence merging), Claude Haiku (identity disambiguation)
  • Media: Google Photos Picker API, ElevenLabs TTS

10.2 API

The Search Star API exposes the following primary resources for institutional and sponsor integrations:

  • GET /api/profile/:id — retrieve public profile (respects visibility settings)
  • GET /api/trust/:id — retrieve Trust record with growth stage and dimension breakdown (requires user authorization)
  • POST /api/sponsorship — create a practice sponsorship pledge
  • POST /api/validation — submit a validator confirmation
  • GET /api/practices/:id — retrieve practice record for a user (respects visibility settings)
  • POST /api/institution/members — bulk-enroll institution members
  • GET /api/institution/analytics — retrieve engagement and Trust stage analytics for institution
  • GET /api/export/:id — export full user data in portable format (requires user authentication)
