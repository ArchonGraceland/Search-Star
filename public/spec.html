1. Overview

Search Star is an open-source standard for self-sovereign personal data profiles. An individual hosts their profile at a URI they control, served as JSON-LD. Platforms — dating apps, recruiters, investment networks, brands — query the individual's API endpoint and pay per query at a price the individual sets.

The standard covers four data domains: financial standing (expressed as age-cohort percentiles, not raw dollars), personal presence (the Presence Composite: Rizz, Vibe, Drip), professional capability (skills, credentials, interests), and an advertising profile (high-value targeting signals the user explicitly approves). Each field can be independently scoped to one of three access tiers.

Profiles are backed by a validation layer where trusted third parties stake money against the accuracy of profile claims. If claims are proven false, validators forfeit their stakes to refund advertisers.

Search Star inverts the data economy. Instead of platforms harvesting user data for free and selling it to advertisers, individuals own, host, price, and monetize their own data directly.
Architectural principle: Search Star is a directory and payment layer, not a hosting platform. Search Star never hosts, stores, or caches user profiles, photos, or media. The user hosts all of their own data on infrastructure they control (see section 8.1). Search Star's database contains only: the directory index (profile numbers, handles, endpoint URLs, trust scores, tier pricing), the earnings ledger (query transactions, revenue splits, settlement records), and the validation layer (validator stakes, automated check results, disputes). If Search Star disappeared tomorrow, every user's profile would still exist at their own URL.

2. Identity & Registration

Each Search Star profile is assigned a permanent, sequential identifier in the format SS-XXXXXX (e.g., SS-000001). This number never changes and is globally unique.

Identity is anchored by a W3C Decentralized Identifier (DID) using the did:web method, resolving to a domain the profile owner controls. KYC verification is required and attested by a third-party provider (e.g., Stripe Identity). The DID, KYC attestation, and profile number together form the identity triple.

3. Profile Schema

Profiles are served as JSON-LD documents at the owner's endpoint. The schema context is https://schema.searchstar.org/v0.3. All fields support field-level access control via the accessTier property.

3.1 Financial (Age-Percentile Model)

All financial metrics are expressed as percentiles within the owner's age cohort (5-year bands). No raw dollar amounts ever leave the profile. This makes a 26-year-old with $100K in savings and a 50-year-old with $2M directly comparable — both might be 90th percentile for their cohort.

Percentile data is sourced from the Federal Reserve Survey of Consumer Finances and verified via zero-knowledge proofs (ZK-SNARKs). The proof attests that the owner's actual value meets or exceeds the claimed percentile threshold, without revealing the underlying number.

3.2 Presence Composite™

Confidence discount

If Presence is scored from self-assessment only (no photos, no peer reviews, no social content), a 0.85 confidence discount is applied to all factor scores. Providing visual evidence (outfit photos, space photos, social content) removes the discount for the relevant dimensions.

Data sources

Presence scores are derived from: photo AI analysis (fit, color, composition), blinded peer reviews (reviewers cannot see others' scores), space photos (home and workspace aesthetic), taste signals (music, film, reading), anonymized conversation samples, and social content originality indexes.

AI photo search & selection New in v0.3

During profile creation, the AI builder should request access to the user's photo library (Google Photos, Apple Photos, or uploaded files) and perform the following analysis:

  1. Photo scan. The AI searches the user's photo collection for images relevant to Presence scoring: outfit shots, group/social photos, travel photos, workspace and living space images, and formal/event photos. The AI should look for variety across contexts (casual, formal, athletic, professional, travel).
  2. Dimension mapping. Each photo is tagged to the Presence dimension it best serves:
    • Drip — outfit shots, full-body photos where clothing is visible, formal event photos
    • Vibe — home/workspace photos, bookshelves, curated environments, travel destinations, cultural events
    • Rizz — group/social photos showing body language and demeanor, candid shots showing natural confidence and warmth
  3. Best photo selection. The AI recommends 6–12 photos that maximize Presence scoring coverage, selecting for:
    • Context variety (at least 2–3 different settings)
    • Recency (prefer photos from the last 2 years)
    • Quality (good lighting, clear subject, uncluttered composition)
    • Dimension coverage (at least 2 photos per Presence dimension)
    • Brand-blind compliance (deprioritize photos with visible logos or brand signaling)
  4. User approval. The AI presents its photo recommendations with explanations of why each was selected and which dimension it serves. The user approves, rejects, or substitutes any photo before it is included in the profile.
  5. Scoring. Approved photos are analyzed for Presence factor scores. Having photo evidence removes the 0.85 confidence discount for the relevant dimensions. Photos covering all three dimensions remove the discount entirely.
Privacy note: The AI searches photos locally during the profile creation session. Photos are never sent to Search Star servers. Selected photos are hosted by the user on their own infrastructure (see section 8.1: Self-Hosting) and referenced by URL in the profile JSON-LD. Search Star indexes only the URL and the hash — never the image data.

3.5 Family & Household New in v0.3

Most platforms that profile people treat career and net worth as the defining signals of a person's value. Search Star deliberately rejects this. Family — building one, raising one, sustaining one — is a first-class section of the profile, equal in structural weight to financial standing, skills, and presence.

This section captures the full picture: existing family, the role the user plays in it, and — for those who want it — their intentions and readiness to build or expand a family. It is designed to make parenthood, caregiving, and family commitment legible as the serious life investment they are, not as a checkbox or a dealbreaker filter.

Design philosophy: A mother raising three children has made a capital allocation, a time commitment, and a values decision at least as significant as founding a company. A father coaching his daughter's soccer team every Saturday is making a choice about how to spend his most finite resource. This section exists so those choices show up in a profile with the same weight and structure as a job title or a net worth percentile.

Builder instructions: When building the family section, the AI should approach this conversationally and warmly. Do not present it as a form. Ask about the user's family life naturally — "Tell me about your family" — and build the structured data from the conversation. For family intentions, be sensitive: not everyone wants children, not everyone can have them, and the section should feel equally welcoming to a single person with no plans for children as to a mother of five. The section is optional. Omitting it carries no stigma and has no effect on the rest of the profile.

3.6 Advertising Profile New in v0.3

The advertising profile makes a user's data valuable to advertisers without exposing them to exploitation. It contains four sub-sections, each composed of signals the user explicitly approves before publication.

Life stage signals

Broad categorical descriptors of where the user is in life. These are inferred from identity, financial, and interests data — the user does not self-categorize into advertising buckets. Each signal includes an advertiserRelevance field that makes the value proposition explicit to querying platforms.

Example signalAdvertiser relevance
Independent self-directed professionalHigh-agency decision-maker, not reachable through employer channels
High-net-worth, low-debt householdPremium financial services, wealth management
Education-involved parentEdTech, curricula, learning platforms
International cultural travelerPremium travel, cultural tourism
Active investorFintech, alternative investments, advisory

Consumption profile

Category-level spending patterns. Never dollar amounts — only qualitative tiers: low, moderate, high, very high. Each entry includes category, frequency, scope, and estimated annual value tier.

Professional openness

What kind of professional inbound the user welcomes: advisory/board roles, consulting, mentorship, collaboration, speaking/teaching, or none. This is asked directly — it cannot be reliably inferred.

Demographic value tags

Shorthand tags for advertising audience segmentation, generated automatically from the profile and approved by the user. Examples: "High-net-worth male 55+", "Cultural luxury segment", "Tech-literate self-directed", "Education decision-maker".

Privacy guardrails. The advertising profile MUST NOT include: medical conditions or health data (insurance discrimination risk), specific financial targets or transaction intentions (front-running risk), or relationship status details beyond broad life stage. The user approves every signal, category, and tag. Nothing is published without explicit consent.

Access control

The adult extension uses a person-level access list managed entirely by the user on their own hosting. The user decides exactly who can see the content, and can revoke any individual's access instantly for any reason — no explanation required, no appeals process, no cooling-off period.

How it works

  1. User grants access. The user adds a person to their access list by email, phone number, or Search Star profile number (SS-XXXXXX). A unique access token is generated for that person and delivered however the user chooses — Marketing tier message, direct message, email, in person. One token per person, scoped to that individual.
  2. Person views content. The person includes their token when requesting the adult extension endpoint. The edge function on the user's hosting checks the token against the access list. Valid token → content served. Invalid or missing token → 403 Forbidden.
  3. User revokes access. The user removes the person from the access list. The token is invalidated immediately. The next request with that token returns 403. No grace period, no notification to the revoked person, no refund if they paid for access. Done.

Access list schema

The access list is a JSON file on the user's hosting, managed through a simple UI provided by the Search Star self-hosting toolkit. The user never needs to edit JSON directly.

Kill switch

The status field at the top level provides a global on/off toggle:

Separately, the user can remove the adultExtension object from their profile JSON-LD entirely, which flips the directory boolean to false on the next crawl and makes the extension undiscoverable. This is the nuclear option — to restore it, they re-add the object and wait for re-indexing.

Reference implementation

Search Star's self-hosting toolkit (see section 8.1) includes a ready-to-deploy access control module for the adult extension. It consists of:

  • An edge function (Cloudflare Worker or Vercel serverless function) that checks tokens against the access list on every request.
  • A simple management UI where the user can add people, remove people, set expirations, flip the kill switch, and see a log of who accessed what and when.
  • No code required from the user. Deploy the template, manage the list through the UI.

Users who prefer to build their own access control can ignore the reference implementation and use any auth mechanism they want — OAuth2, HTTP basic auth, IP allowlists, or anything else. The only requirement is that the endpoint returns content for authorized requests and 403 for unauthorized ones. Search Star doesn't care how the user implements access control — only that the user has it.

Consent framework

Explicit content on Search Star operates on a strict pull-only, consent-first model. No one ever receives explicit content they didn't actively request. The system is designed so that unsolicited explicit material — the "dick pic problem" — is structurally impossible, not just discouraged.

How consent works end-to-end

  1. Discovery. A person sees that a profile has an adult extension (the boolean flag in the directory). This tells them the extension exists — nothing about what's in it. No preview, no thumbnail, no description is exposed in the directory or search results.
  2. Request. If the person wants access, they send a Marketing tier message to the profile owner. This message is plain text only (see section 4.3) — no images, no links to explicit content, no media of any kind. The message might say "I'd like access to your adult extension" or nothing at all — the content of the request is up to the sender. The sender pays the owner's Marketing tier price, which filters out spam and signals seriousness.
  3. Grant. The profile owner reviews the request, sees the sender's verified identity (KYC'd), and decides whether to grant access. If yes, the owner generates a token and delivers it to the requester. If no, nothing happens — the owner can ignore the request entirely or block the sender.
  4. Access. The requester uses their token to view the adult extension at the owner's self-hosted endpoint. They go to the content — the content never comes to them. Every view is a deliberate act by the viewer.
  5. Revocation. The owner can revoke access at any time for any reason. Instant, unilateral, no explanation required.

What is structurally prevented

Attack vectorHow it's blocked
Sending explicit images via Marketing tier messageMarketing messages are plain text only. Media payloads are rejected at the API level. Links to explicit content are flagged and result in account suspension.
Pushing explicit content to someone's inboxThere is no content push mechanism in Search Star. The adult extension is a self-hosted endpoint that people visit — it does not send content to anyone.
Embedding explicit content in a profile query responseProfile queries return structured JSON-LD. The adult extension is a separate endpoint, not part of the standard profile response. Platforms never receive adult content through the Search Star API.
Using the content feed to distribute explicit materialContent feeds are metered through Search Star's payment infrastructure and subject to Stripe's content policies. Explicit content in feeds would violate Stripe ToS and result in account termination. Explicit content belongs in the adult extension only — self-hosted, outside Search Star's infrastructure.
Spamming access tokens to people who didn't request themA token is useless without the intent to use it. Receiving an unsolicited token doesn't expose anyone to content — they'd have to actively visit the endpoint and submit the token. An unwanted token is just a meaningless string.
The principle: initiative belongs to the viewer. At every step — discovery, request, access — the person viewing explicit content is the one taking action. The content owner can grant or deny access, but they cannot force content onto anyone. This is the opposite of how unsolicited explicit content works on traditional platforms, where the sender pushes an image into someone's inbox or DMs without consent. On Search Star, you have to go get it. It never comes to you.
Your content, your rules. Revoking access is instant and unilateral. The user owes no explanation. There is no dispute process, no appeal, and no refund. This is not a bug — it is the core design principle. The person granted access accepted these terms by requesting access in the first place. If the user wants to shut someone out at 3am because they feel like it, the system supports that with a single tap.
Why the separation? Keeping adult content off Search Star's infrastructure protects the core platform's payment processing (Stripe prohibits explicit content on managed accounts), app store compliance, and mainstream platform partnerships. Users maintain full freedom to host whatever they want on their own domain — the spec simply doesn't route that content through Search Star's systems.

3.8 Content Feed New in v0.4

The content feed turns a Search Star profile into a sovereign publishing channel. The user hosts a feed of content — essays, analysis, photos, podcasts, video, newsletters, or any media — at their own domain. Platforms and individuals access the feed through Search Star's payment layer, paying per item or via subscription. The same 90/10 revenue split applies.

This is the inverse of social media. Instead of the platform owning the feed and selling the audience to advertisers, the person owns the feed and sells access directly. There is no algorithmic amplification, no engagement optimization, no injected content from strangers, and no ads. Every item costs real money to access, so the content has to be worth it. The slop disappears because there is no incentive to manufacture it.

Architecture

The content feed follows the same sovereignty model as the rest of Search Star: the user hosts the feed at their own endpoint, and Search Star indexes only metadata in the directory. Content never passes through or is cached by Search Star's infrastructure — the feed endpoint is fetched on demand when a paid query executes, exactly like a profile query.

4.4 Payment Infrastructure New in v0.3

Search Star handles the flow of money between querying platforms and profile owners. The system uses Stripe Connect as the payment rail, with prepaid credit balances for platforms and weekly batch settlement for profile owners.

How money flows

The payment cycle has four participants: the querying platform, Search Star (the marketplace), the profile owner, and Stripe (the payment processor).

  1. Platform funds account. A querying platform (e.g., a dating app, a recruiter, a brand) creates a Search Star API account and deposits funds into a prepaid credit balance. This works like a toll pass — the platform loads credits in advance, and queries draw down the balance automatically.
  2. Query executes. When a platform queries a profile, the system checks the platform's credit balance. If sufficient funds exist, the query is served and the balance is debited by the profile owner's price for that tier. If insufficient funds, the query is rejected with a 402 Payment Required response.
  3. Revenue splits. Each query's revenue is split immediately upon execution: 90% is credited to the profile owner's earnings ledger, and 10% is credited to Search Star's revenue account.
  4. Weekly settlement. Every Monday at 00:00 UTC, all accumulated profile owner earnings are settled via Stripe Connect payouts to the owner's linked bank account. Minimum payout threshold: $1.00. Earnings below the threshold roll over to the next week.

When a query arrives, the following happens in a single atomic transaction:

  2. Look up the profile owner's price for that tier.
  3. Check the platform's credit balance ≥ the query price.
  4. All debits are atomic. If the profile data cannot be served (e.g., the owner's hosting is down), the debit is not executed. Platforms are never charged for failed queries.

4.8 Feed Subscriptions New in v0.4

Feed subscriptions are recurring prepaid commitments that give a platform or individual access to all new content a profile owner publishes during the billing period. Subscriptions are managed through Search Star's payment infrastructure using Stripe Connect recurring billing.

How subscriptions work

  1. Platform creates a subscription. POST /v1/subscribe/{profileNumber}/feed — the platform's credit balance is debited for the first interval immediately.
  2. Access granted. The subscriber receives a subscription ID and can query any feed item published after the subscription start date without additional per-item charges.
  3. Recurring billing. At each interval boundary (e.g., monthly), Search Star automatically debits the platform's credit balance for the next period. If the balance is insufficient and auto-refill fails, the subscription is paused — not cancelled. It resumes when funds are available.
  4. Revenue split. The same 90/10 split applies to each subscription payment. On a $0.50/month subscription: $0.45 to the owner, $0.05 to Search Star.
  5. Settlement. Subscription revenue is credited to the owner's earnings ledger at the moment of each interval debit and included in the standard weekly Monday settlement.

Subscriptions don't bypass sovereignty. A subscription grants the right to query feed items — it does not grant the right to cache, redistribute, or archive content. Each item access still flows through Search Star's payment layer and is logged. If the owner removes an item from their feed endpoint, it becomes inaccessible to subscribers. The owner retains full control of their content at all times.

5. Validation & Trust Layer New in v0.3

Search Star's trust system is built on a single insight: trust is not a status — trust is an action. You demonstrate trust when you're willing to put your reputation on the line to validate someone else's information. Every member is a potential validator within their network. Trust is measured by what people are willing to vouch for. Reputation — not money alone — is the cost of endorsement.

Architectural principle: Search Star is a directory and payment layer, not a hosting platform. Search Star never hosts, stores, or caches user profiles, photos, or media. The user hosts all of their own data on infrastructure they control (see section 8.1). Search Star's database contains only: the directory index (profile numbers, handles, endpoint URLs, trust scores, tier pricing), the earnings ledger (query transactions, revenue splits, settlement records), and the validation layer (validator relationships, trust scores, affiliate associations, disputes). If Search Star disappeared tomorrow, every user's profile would still exist at their own URL.

5.1 Trust as Validation (Core Philosophy) New in v0.9

Garbage culture — AI slop, influencer fraud, fake outrage, clickbait — is fundamentally a trust crisis. Every form of garbage is an unverified claim with no one willing to vouch for it. AI slop claims authorship it doesn't have. Influencers claim lifestyles never lived. Clickbait claims value it can't deliver. In every case, no human is willing to stake their reputation on the claim's accuracy.

Search Star's structural defense is that every meaningful claim on a profile can be validated by a real person who's willing to put their name on it. The cost of garbage isn't just monetary — it's reputational, because someone real had to vouch for it. This is enforced by architecture, not moderation policies or AI content filters.

The system produces trust through sustained effort in real communities. You can't validate someone you don't actually know. You can't know someone without spending time with them in shared activities. Therefore, the communities that produce the most valuable validators are those requiring ongoing effort and genuine participation: athletic clubs, intellectual societies, creative ensembles, religious congregations, and — most fundamentally — extended family networks.

5.6 Dispute Resolution

When information is proven wrong, the following process applies:

  1. Automated check fails. If a previously-passing automated check returns a contradictory result on re-verification, the system flags the field automatically.
  2. Platform dispute. A querying platform that paid for profile data can file a dispute with evidence of material inaccuracy.
  3. Review. Automated-source contradictions are upheld automatically. Subjective disputes are flagged for manual review.
  4. Penalty on upheld dispute: The field is marked as disputed. Validators who vouched for it forfeit their stakes: 70% refunded to querying platforms, 20% platform fee, 10% to the disputing party. The validator's own Trust Score takes a hit via the accuracy tracking mechanism (see section 5.9).
  5. Bad-faith dispute penalty. If a dispute is rejected (the profile data was accurate), the disputing party pays a $5 filing fee to deter frivolous disputes.
The profile owner may update any disputed field and request re-verification. The Trust Score is recalculated after every dispute resolution.

5.8 Referral Validation & Revenue Sharing

Individual users (not just organizations) can recruit new users into the Search Star network and earn a perpetual 5% share of the recruited user's query revenue — in exchange for staking money against the accuracy of that user's profile. This creates a decentralized growth engine: recruiters are financially incentivized to bring in real people with truthful profiles, and penalized for onboarding fraudulent ones.

How referral validation works

  1. Recruiter invites a new user. Any existing Search Star user with a Validator Reputation Score ≥ 60 can generate a referral link. The link is tied to the recruiter's profile number.
  2. New user signs up via the referral link. Normal registration (section 7.1). The referral link associates the recruiter as the referringValidator.
  3. Recruiter validates the new user's profile. The recruiter must complete a full validation and stake minimum $5.00. Revenue share does not activate until staking is complete.
  4. Revenue share activates. Recruiter earns 5% of the new user's net query revenue. Owner receives 85%, Search Star 10%.

The 5% share continues indefinitely as long as the recruiter maintains an active validation stake. If the recruiter withdraws their stake, revenue share stops immediately. The owner can terminate the referral relationship at any time.

There are no uplines, downlines, recruitment bonuses, or compounding tiers. The recruiter must stake real money and is financially penalized for validating bad data.

5.11 Validator Cooperative New in v1.0

Validators are the backbone of Search Star's trust infrastructure. The Validator Cooperative formalizes this by granting qualifying validators revenue participation and governance rights — structured as an LLC membership class that avoids securities classification.

Legal structure

Search Star operates as a multi-class LLC. The Operating Class holds full management authority over product, engineering, hiring, pricing, and business strategy. The Validator Member Class holds revenue participation rights and limited governance rights scoped exclusively to trust-layer policy. Validator Members are classified as service participants, not passive investors — their revenue share is tied to ongoing validation work, not a capital contribution. This structure is designed to avoid securities registration under the Howey test, because returns depend on the validator's own efforts, not solely the efforts of others.

Not equity, not a security. Validator membership is a revenue-sharing arrangement tied to active service. It does not confer ownership of Search Star's intellectual property, brand, or technology. It cannot be sold, transferred, or inherited. If a validator stops qualifying, their membership and revenue share terminate. Legal counsel should review the operating agreement in each jurisdiction where validators are active.

Qualification criteria

Validator Cooperative membership is earned, not purchased. A validator qualifies when all of the following are met:

Each qualifying member's share of the quarterly pool is calculated as:

member_score = active_stake × validated_profile_count × months_active
member_share = member_score / sum(all_member_scores)
member_payout = validator_revenue_pool × member_share

This rewards validators who stake more, validate more profiles, and participate over longer periods. A validator with $2,000 staked across 8 profiles over 18 months receives a substantially larger share than one with $500 across 3 profiles over 6 months.

Governance rights

Validator Members vote on trust-layer policy questions. Governance is limited in scope — members do not vote on product direction, pricing, hiring, technology, or business strategy. Voting is weighted by the same stake-weighted formula used for revenue allocation.

Revenue pool guardrails. Validator Members can vote to adjust the pool percentage, but only within a 10–30% band of marketplace fee revenue. This prevents the cooperative from voting itself an unsustainable share while still giving members meaningful control over their compensation.

Voting mechanics

Votes are conducted quarterly, coinciding with revenue distributions. Proposals can be submitted by any member with ≥ 12 months tenure. A proposal passes with a simple majority of stake-weighted votes, with a minimum quorum of 25% of total member stake. Results are published transparently to all members.

Membership termination

Membership is automatically suspended if a validator falls below qualification thresholds. Specifically:

  1. Reputation Score drops below 60. Membership suspended. Revenue share paused. If score recovers above 70 within 6 months, membership reinstates automatically.
  2. Upheld dispute. Membership suspended for 12 months from the dispute resolution date. Must re-qualify after suspension.
  3. Inactivity. If a member has zero active validations for 6 consecutive months, membership lapses. Must re-qualify from scratch.
  4. Voluntary withdrawal. A member may resign at any time. Accrued but unpaid distributions are paid out in the next quarterly cycle.

Terminated members retain no ongoing rights. There is no buyout, no residual claim, and no transferability. Membership is personal and non-assignable.

The cooperative's narrative. "The people who keep Search Star honest own a piece of it." Validator cooperative membership is designed to be a genuine badge of honor — a recognition that this person has staked real money, maintained accuracy, and participated in the trust infrastructure over a sustained period. It is the highest status a non-operator can hold in the Search Star ecosystem.

7. Directory, Registration & Search API New in v0.3

Search Star is a directory and payment layer. Users host their own profiles. Platforms discover profiles through Search Star's directory and search API, then query user-hosted endpoints directly — with payment routed through Search Star. This section specifies how profiles get into the directory, how they're indexed, what the directory stores, and how platforms search it.

7.1 Profile Registration

After a user creates their profile (via the AI builder) and hosts it at their own domain (see section 8.1), they register the profile endpoint with Search Star.

Registration flow

  1. User creates a Search Star account. Email + password via Supabase Auth. This is the user's account for managing their directory listing, earnings, and validators — not for hosting their profile.
  2. User submits their profile endpoint URL. Example: https://yourname.com/profile.json
  3. Search Star validates the endpoint. The system fetches the URL and checks:
    • Returns valid JSON-LD with @context: "https://schema.searchstar.org/v0.3"
    • Contains required fields: identity.displayName, identity.handle, accessPolicy
    • Served over HTTPS
    • Response time under 5 seconds
  4. Domain ownership verification. The user proves they control the domain by adding a DNS TXT record: searchstar-verify=SS-XXXXXX. Alternatively, they can place a verification file at /.well-known/searchstar-verification.json.
  5. Profile number assigned. Upon successful validation and domain verification, Search Star assigns the next sequential profile number (SS-XXXXXX) and indexes the profile metadata.
  6. User connects Stripe. To receive earnings from queries, the user connects a Stripe Connect account. This can be done at registration or later — the profile is discoverable in the directory either way, but queries that would generate revenue require Stripe to be connected.
Handle uniqueness. The identity.handle in the user's profile JSON-LD must be unique across the directory. If a handle is already claimed, registration fails until the user changes their handle. Handles are case-insensitive and follow the format @name or name.domain.tld.

Recommended self-hosting options

      
Users need a domain they control and a place to serve static files (JSON-LD, images). Most Search Star users are not developers, so the default recommendation prioritizes simplicity.


      
Endpoint URL is never exposed to platforms. When a user registers a self-hosted endpoint URL, that URL is stored in the Search Star directory as an internal field. It is never returned in any API response to querying platforms. When a platform executes a paid query via POST /v1/query/{id}, Search Star's backend fetches the profile from the user's endpoint server-side, filters the response to the requested tier, and serves the result. The platform receives only the filtered profile data — never the raw endpoint URL. This means a plain static JSON-LD file works with no authentication required on the user's hosting. The payment layer cannot be bypassed because platforms cannot discover the endpoint to query it directly. Upgrade path: OAuth 2.0 token-based authentication between Search Star and user endpoints is planned for v2.0.

What to look for in a host

      
    
        
  • Custom domain support — your profile should live at a URL you own (e.g., profile.yourname.com), not a platform subdomain.
    • 
        
  • HTTPS by default — querying platforms require it. All recommended options provide free SSL.
    • 
        
  • Global CDN — dating apps and recruiters need fast response times. Cloudflare, Vercel, and Netlify all include this.
    • 
        
  • Photo storage with direct URLs — each photo needs a stable, publicly accessible URL. Cloudflare R2 and S3 do this natively. Vercel and Netlify serve photos from the repo/deploy.
    • 
        
  • Uptime — if your hosting is down, queries fail and you earn nothing. Choose infrastructure with 99.9%+ uptime.
    • 
        
  • You can delete everything — true sovereignty means you can remove your profile and all photos at any time with no holdback period.
    • 
      


      
Photo hosting requirements

      
Selected profile photos must be hosted at URLs the user controls, served over HTTPS, and accessible to querying platforms without authentication (for photos in the public or private tier). The profile JSON-LD references each photo by URL and SHA-256 content hash. Querying platforms verify the hash to ensure photos haven't been tampered with since scoring.

      
Recommended image specs: WebP or JPEG format, minimum 800px on the short edge, maximum 5MB per image. Photos should be served with appropriate cache headers (minimum 24h cache) for query performance.


      
Search Star never stores your photos. When the AI builder recommends photos during profile creation, the analysis happens in the AI session (e.g., Claude analyzing uploaded images). The photos are then placed on your hosting by you. Search Star's directory stores only the URL and the hash — if you delete a photo from your hosting, it disappears from your profile. You maintain full control at all times.


      

      
8.2 Personal Page & Visibility Modes New in v0.8

      
Each user hosts two files at their domain: profile.json (structured data for machines) and index.html (a personal page for humans). The JSON-LD file is the API endpoint that Search Star crawls and platforms query. The HTML page is a self-contained personal landing page that anyone can visit by navigating to the user's domain.


      
Two files, two audiences

      

        
FileAudiencePurpose

        
profile.jsonMachinesSearch Star's crawler reads this to index directory metadata. Platforms query it through the paid API. The endpoint URL is internal to Search Star and never exposed to platforms.

        
index.htmlHumansAnyone who visits the user's domain sees this page. It displays the profile visually — photos, scores, interests — in the Graceland design system. The user controls how much it reveals.

      


      
Visibility modes

      
The personal page supports two visibility modes, chosen at profile creation time. The AI builder asks the user which mode they prefer and generates the HTML accordingly.

FullName, photos, Presence Composite (Rizz/Vibe/Drip with visual bars), financial percentiles, skills, interests, tagline, location. SummaryName, tagline, one photo (optional). No detailed scores, no financial data, no skills breakdown. A "View Full Profile on Search Star" button links to the directory. In both modes, the profile.json file at the same domain contains the full structured data. The visibility mode controls only what the human-readable HTML page displays. Platforms querying through the API always get the tier-appropriate data regardless of the personal page visibility mode — the two are independent.


      
Privacy is not obscurity. The profile.json file is technically accessible to anyone who knows the URL. However, Search Star never reveals the endpoint URL to platforms — they can only access profile data through the paid query API. The URL is stored as an internal field in the directory. For users who want stronger protection, the v2.0 roadmap includes OAuth 2.0 token-based authentication between Search Star and user endpoints, so the JSON file itself would reject unauthenticated requests.


      

      
8.3 Contact via Search Star New in v0.8

      
Every personal page — in both full and summary visibility modes — includes a prominent "Contact Me via Search Star" button. This is the only contact mechanism exposed on the page. No email address, no phone number, no social media DMs, no contact form.


      
How it works

      
The button links to Search Star's login page (in the MVP) or to a deep-link for the profile's marketing tier (in future versions). When someone clicks it, they are directed to create a Search Star platform account, fund it with credits, and send a marketing message at the profile owner's set price. The owner receives the message in their feed and gets paid the marketing tier price minus the 10% marketplace fee.


      
Design principle

      
The personal page is a storefront, not an open door. Visitors can see who the user is — as much or as little as the user chooses via the visibility mode — but the only way to make contact is through Search Star's paid messaging layer. The user's attention has a price. The button enforces it.


      
This solves three problems simultaneously:

      
    
        
  • Spam. Every message costs real money. The price filters out mass outreach, bots, and low-effort contact attempts.
    • 
        
  • Privacy. No personal contact information is exposed on the public web. The user's email, phone, and other identifiers stay off the page entirely.
    • 
        
  • Monetization. Every inbound contact generates revenue for the profile owner. The personal page becomes a revenue channel, not just a digital business card.
    • 
      


      
Required element. The "Contact Me via Search Star" button is a mandatory element of the personal page. The AI builder includes it automatically in both visibility modes. Users may style or position it however they like, but they may not remove it — a personal page without a Search Star contact path is not a valid Search Star profile page. This ensures that every profile in the directory has a functioning inbound contact channel.


      

      
8.4 AI-Assisted Profile Creation New in v0.8

      
Search Star profiles are built using an AI assistant. The user copies a prompt package from searchstar.com/create.html, pastes it into any AI (Claude, ChatGPT, Grok, Gemini), and the AI conducts a conversational interview that produces both the profile.json and index.html files.


      
The creation flow

      
    
        
  1. Copy the prompt. The prompt package at /create.html contains the complete JSON-LD schema, scoring methodology, design guidelines for the HTML page, visibility mode instructions, and the "Contact via Search Star" button requirement.
    2. 
        
  2. Paste into any AI. The user pastes the prompt into their AI of choice. If the AI has file access (e.g., Claude Projects with uploaded documents), it can search the user's existing data — financial exports, fitness apps, photos, resumes — to populate the profile automatically.
    3. 
        
  3. Conversational interview. The AI walks through each section: identity, financial percentiles, Presence Composite (Rizz/Vibe/Drip), skills, interests, photos, pricing, and visibility mode. It asks questions, analyzes uploaded data, and builds both files incrementally.
    4. 
        
  4. Review and approve. The user reviews every field before the AI finalizes. Nothing is published without explicit approval.
    5. 
        
  5. Download two files. The AI outputs profile.json and index.html. The user saves both to a folder along with their selected photos.
    6. 
        
  6. Host the folder. The user uploads the folder to Cloudflare Pages, Vercel, Netlify, or any static hosting. The hosting guide is integrated into the creation page.
    7. 
        
  7. Register with Search Star. The user signs up at searchstar.com, enters their endpoint URL at /profile-builder, and Search Star validates the JSON-LD, extracts directory metadata, and assigns the permanent profile number (SS-XXXXXX).
    8.

9. Platform Portal Implemented in v0.8

      
The Platform Portal is the demand side of Search Star. While profile owners manage their data and earnings from the owner dashboard, platforms — dating apps, recruiters, brands, investment networks, and any entity that wants to query profiles or send marketing messages — manage their operations from a separate portal.

      
The portal exists because the spec (sections 4.4, 7.4, 7.5) defines a complete platform-side workflow — prepaid credits, API keys, directory search, per-query billing, marketing messages — but without a management interface, that workflow is headless. The Platform Portal provides the UI for every action a querying platform needs to take.


      
Two-sided marketplace, two separate experiences. Profile owners and platforms never share a login or a dashboard. An owner's dashboard shows earnings, messages received, and profile stats. A platform's portal shows spending, queries executed, messages sent, and credit balance. The data is the same ledger viewed from opposite sides.


      
Implementation status (v0.8): The Platform Portal is live at searchstar.com/platform. Platform signup at /platform-signup. The portal uses a teal accent (#0d9488) on a dark teal sidebar (#0f2e2b) to visually distinguish it from the owner dashboard (navy) and admin panel (dark). All features described in sections 9.1–9.4 are implemented in the MVP, with Stripe integration deferred to a mock deposit system. The directory browser, paid query execution (with 90/10 revenue split), marketing message composer, and spending analytics are fully functional.


      
9.1 Platform Accounts

      
A platform account is distinct from a profile owner account. Platforms sign up with a company name, billing email, and company URL. They do not create Search Star profiles — they query them.
