# How the Sponsorship Model Works

**Target duration:** 3–4 minutes
**Target word count:** 510 words
**Audio file:** /audio/video-02-sponsorship-model.mp3

---

The sponsorship model on Search Star is built around one principle: you keep everything you earn.

When sponsors pledge support during your 14-day launch window, those pledges are yours. Search Star takes no cut. When you complete your 90-day streak and the payout triggers, the full pledged amount goes to you. Payment processing passes through at cost. That's the only mandatory fee.

So how does Search Star sustain itself? Through voluntary contributions.

Here's how that works. At the moment your streak completes and funds are released, a single contribution prompt appears. It suggests a portion — 50% of your payout is the default — and shows exactly where that contribution would go: a small share to Search Star, the rest flowing through the mentor economy to the mentor, coach, community builder, and practice leader roles that make the platform function.

You can reduce that amount. You can redirect it. You can remove it entirely with one action. There is no follow-up prompt. No guilt. No dark pattern.

This is the GoFundMe model. GoFundMe operates the same way and reports that roughly 70% of campaigns receive voluntary contributions even when they're entirely optional. That number makes sense. People who have just seen a real goal completed — who backed someone they believed in and watched them actually finish — are in a moment of genuine satisfaction. Contributing at that moment isn't a tax. It's an expression of the experience.

The economics work because the payout amounts are meaningful. The target payout for a completed 90-day streak is $2,500. Two streaks per year means a practitioner can earn up to $5,000. That's real money — not a coffee-shop side hustle, not a discount on a premium tier. Money that reflects the genuine effort of three months of sustained practice.

At those payout levels, even a modest voluntary contribution rate funds living wages for the people who make the platform worth joining. A mentor who helps real practitioners build real practices earns from every payout moment in their community. Not a salary. Not a guaranteed income. A share of the outcomes they helped create.

The split is transparent before you confirm. You see the numbers. You can adjust them. The default is a suggestion, not a contract.

For institutions deploying Search Star as a benefit program, the model is simpler still. The institution allocates a sponsorship budget to its community. Members earn from that budget by completing validated practice goals. The institution pays nothing beyond what was pledged — no platform licensing fee, no per-seat charge, no mandatory subscription.

This is unusual. Most platforms charge for access and treat users as the product. Search Star charges only for outcomes it helped produce, and only as much as people freely choose to give.

The bet is straightforward: a platform that creates genuine value should be able to sustain itself through the freely given support of the people it serves.

So far, that bet is holding.
